Excel & Power BI Intro

Learn to interrogate AI-generated Excel formulas and Power BI DAX measures. Understand the difference between Google Sheets and Excel, and why AI often confuses the two.

Concept Explanation

Important distinction: 49.7% of our community uses Google Sheets, but employers expect Excel proficiency (71.3%). AI often confuses the two — and the formulas are different!

Power BI & DAX Basics

DAX (Data Analysis Expressions) is the formula language for Power BI. It's similar to Excel formulas but works on entire columns, not individual cells.

# Basic DAX measure
Total Sales = SUM(Sales[Amount])

AI-Generated Answer (What ChatGPT/Copilot Would Output)

Here's what an AI would generate if asked: "Write a formula to look up customer names from a table based on customer ID."

# AI-generated formula (Excel)
=XLOOKUP(A2, CustomerID[CustomerID], CustomerID[Name])

This looks correct for Excel. But what if you're using Google Sheets? Let's find out.

Where AI Gets It Wrong

This formula has three common pitfalls that AI often misses:

  1. 1
    XLOOKUP doesn't work in Google Sheets. If you're using Google Sheets (49.7% of our community), this formula will return a #NAME? error. Use VLOOKUP or QUERY() instead.
  2. 2
    No handling of #N/A errors. If the customer ID doesn't exist, XLOOKUP returns #N/A. Wrap it in IFNA() to show a friendly message instead.
  3. 3
    DAX measure context matters. If this DAX formula is used in a Power BI report with date filters, it won't respect the filter context unless you use CALCULATE(). AI rarely explains this.
# Fixed version for Google Sheets
=IFNA(VLOOKUP(A2, CustomerID!A:B, 2, FALSE), "Customer not found")

# Fixed version for DAX (respects filter context)
Total Sales = CALCULATE(SUM(Sales[Amount]))

How a Practitioner Validates This Formula

Before trusting any AI-generated Excel/DAX formula, practitioners follow these validation steps:

  1. 1
    Identify the platform. Is this Excel or Google Sheets? The formula syntax differs significantly.
  • 2
    Test with known data. Enter a few rows of known input/output and verify the formula produces correct results.
  • 3
    Test edge cases. What happens with empty cells? Duplicate IDs? Text vs number mismatches?
  • 4
    Check error handling. Does the formula show #N/A, #VALUE!, or a friendly message?
  • 5
    For DAX: verify filter context. Does the measure respond to slicers, date filters, and row context correctly?
